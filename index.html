1. Abstract

The integration of virtual reality (VR) in educational settings offers immense potential to enhance learning outcomes and student engagement. However, its quantifiable impact on cognitive skill development, such as problem-solving, remains underexplored. This research bridges a critical gap by providing empirical evidence on VR’s effectiveness in enhancing problem-solving skills within a medical education context. Using Polya’s Problem-Solving Framework, the study evaluates cognitive improvements in a high-stakes scenario—postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)—by comparing VR-based and traditional teaching methods. Results will pioneer a framework for assessing immersive technologies in education, advancing their application across critical domains.

7. Contributions to Literature

This study advances VR research by addressing a critical gap in empirical data on its cognitive impact. Beyond engagement metrics, it quantifies VR’s effect on problem-solving, providing actionable insights for educators and policymakers. The results could inform scalable VR integrations in medical curricula, promoting evidence-based teaching practices.



8. Future Directions

This research lays the groundwork for expanding VR’s application across disciplines. Future studies could explore its impact on other cognitive domains, such as critical thinking and decision-making, or adapt the framework to fields like engineering and business. The development of standardized VR evaluation tools will further enhance its integration into diverse educational contexts.



9. Conclusion

This study provides a rigorous framework for assessing VR’s impact on problem-solving skills in medical education. By comparing VR-based and traditional training methods using Polya’s Problem-Solving Framework, the research generates empirical evidence critical for advancing immersive technologies in education. The anticipated findings underscore VR’s transformative potential, paving the way for its broader adoption in training programs across high-stakes fields.
